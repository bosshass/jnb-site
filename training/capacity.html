Capacity & Scaling

When to hire (and when NOT to). The ceiling math.

Your Revenue Has a Ceiling

There's a hard limit on how much money your current team can generate. It's not about motivation or effort — it's math:

Maximum Revenue = People × Billable Hours × Realized Rate

Until you change one of those variables, you cannot grow past that number. Period.

The Ceiling Calculation

Example

3 technicians, 60% utilization, $85 realized rate:

  • Available hours per tech: 2,080/year
  • Billable hours per tech: 2,080 × 60% = 1,248/year
  • Revenue per tech: 1,248 × $85 = $106,080/year
  • Total capacity: 3 × $106,080 = $318,240/year

That's your ceiling. You cannot bill $400K with this team at these utilization and rate levels.

When to Hire

Hire when you're consistently hitting 85%+ of capacity and have enough backlog to keep a new person busy. Hiring at 60% capacity means you're adding cost without adding revenue.

The Hiring Math

New hire cost (first year): $55,000 (wages + taxes + training + ramp-up)

Revenue potential (at 60% utilization, $85 rate): $106,080

Net contribution: $51,080 — IF they're fully utilized

If utilization drops to 40% during ramp-up, that hire costs you money for 6-12 months before they break even.

When NOT to Hire

  • Utilization is below 70%: You have unused capacity — fix that first
  • Realized rate is declining: Adding people amplifies the problem
  • No backlog: You're hiring on hope, not demand
  • Cash reserves < 3 months: One slow month kills you

Subs vs. W2

Subcontractors cost more per hour but have zero fixed cost when there's no work. W2 employees cost less per hour but cost you whether they're working or not.

Rule of thumb: Use subs for overflow and variable demand. Use W2 for consistent, predictable workload.

Bottom Line

  • Capacity = People × Billable Hours × Realized Rate
  • Hire when you're at 85%+ capacity with consistent backlog
  • Fix utilization before adding headcount
  • Every hire should have a clear path to paying for themselves
