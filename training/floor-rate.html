Floor Rate

The minimum you need to charge per hour just to break even. Most business owners have no idea what theirs is.

The $25/Hour Mirage

You pay a tech $25/hour. So you bill $50/hour and think you're making great margin. You're not.

That $25/hour employee actually costs you closer to $35-40/hour when you add payroll taxes, workers' comp, benefits, training time, and overhead allocation. And they're not billing 40 hours a week — they're billing maybe 25-30 after drive time, callbacks, admin work, and sick days.

The Real Math

Here's what floor rate actually looks like:

True Cost = Wages + Payroll Taxes + Benefits + Training + Overhead Allocation

Available Hours = 2,080 - PTO - Holidays - Sick Days - Non-billable Time

Floor Rate = True Cost ÷ Available Billable Hours

For most service businesses, this number is 40-50% higher than the hourly wage you're paying.

Example

Employee making $25/hour:

  • Annual wages: $52,000
  • Payroll taxes (7.65%): $3,978
  • Workers' comp (est.): $2,600
  • Benefits/training: $3,000
  • Overhead allocation: $6,000
  • True annual cost: $67,578

Available billable hours: 2,080 - 80 (PTO) - 56 (holidays) - 40 (sick) - 426 (non-billable) = 1,478 hours

Floor rate: $67,578 ÷ 1,478 = $45.72/hour

Bill below $45.72 and you're losing money on every hour, no matter how "busy" you feel.

Why This Matters

Most service businesses set prices based on what competitors charge, what feels "fair," or what clients will accept without pushback. None of these have anything to do with whether you make money.

Floor rate gives you a hard boundary: below this number, you're subsidizing your clients' businesses with your own. Above it, you're starting to build margin.

Bottom Line

  • Floor rate = True Cost ÷ Available Billable Hours
  • It's always higher than the hourly wage — often 40-50% higher
  • Billing below floor = guaranteed loss, no matter how "busy" you are
  • You can't set prices, evaluate jobs, or plan hiring until you know this number
