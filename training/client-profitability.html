Client Profitability

Not all clients are created equal. Some make you money. Some cost you money.

Your Best Client Might Be Your Worst

That client who gives you the most revenue? They might actually be losing you money. Revenue isn't profit. And until you calculate profit by client, you're flying blind.

The Client Profitability Calculation

Client Profit = Revenue from Client - (Hours Spent × Floor Rate) - Direct Costs

Example: Two Clients

Client A: $8,000/month revenue

  • Hours spent: 100 (including unbilled coordination, callbacks, meetings)
  • Floor rate: $50/hour
  • Labor cost: $5,000
  • Materials/direct costs: $1,500
  • Profit: $1,500 (18.75% margin)

Client B: $4,000/month revenue

  • Hours spent: 35 (efficient, no callbacks, pays on time)
  • Floor rate: $50/hour
  • Labor cost: $1,750
  • Materials/direct costs: $800
  • Profit: $1,450 (36.25% margin)

Client A generates twice the revenue but almost identical profit — and uses 3x the capacity. Client B is objectively more valuable to your business.

The Hidden Costs

When calculating client profitability, don't forget:

  • Unbilled time: Emails, calls, coordination, scope creep
  • Payment delays: Net-60 clients cost you cash flow
  • Callbacks/warranty: Time spent fixing problems
  • Opportunity cost: What else could that capacity generate?
  • Stress tax: Difficult clients burn out your team

The "Fire This Client" Math

A client is costing you money if:

  • Realized rate for that client is below your floor rate
  • They consume capacity that could serve more profitable clients
  • They create unbillable work that doesn't show up on invoices
  • Your team dreads working with them (turnover is expensive)

Firing a bad client creates capacity for a good one. That's not losing revenue — that's making room for profit.

Ranking Your Clients

Every quarter, rank your clients by:

  • Profit margin % (not revenue)
  • Realized rate (revenue ÷ actual hours)
  • Payment speed (cash flow impact)
  • Growth potential (can this relationship expand?)

Your bottom 20% is where you start raising rates, reducing scope, or having the hard conversation.

Bottom Line

  • Revenue ≠ Profit — calculate both by client
  • Include ALL time spent, not just billable hours
  • High-revenue clients can be low-profit clients
  • Firing bad clients creates room for good ones
  • Rank clients quarterly and act on the bottom 20%

You Made It

You now understand the six numbers that drive profitability in a service business:

  • Floor Rate: Your break-even per hour
  • Survival Number: What the business needs to stay alive
  • Utilization: Billable vs. available hours
  • Realized Rate: What you actually collect
  • Capacity: Your revenue ceiling
  • Client Profitability: Which clients make you money

These aren't accounting concepts. They're decision-making tools. Know them, track them, and you'll stop guessing and start building.
