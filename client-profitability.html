Client Profitability

Your biggest client might not be your most profitable

Revenue ≠ Profit

You have a client that brings in $120K/year. They must be important, right?

Maybe. Or maybe they're consuming 40% of your capacity at a 15% margin while a $30K client delivers 45% margin with zero drama.

You can't know until you measure.

How to Calculate Client Profitability

Client Profit = Revenue - (Hours × Loaded Cost) - Direct Expenses

For each client, you need:

  • Total revenue from that client
  • Total hours spent (all hours, not just billed)
  • Your loaded cost per hour (from floor rate)
  • Any direct expenses (materials, travel, subcontractors)

What You'll Find

When service businesses do this analysis for the first time, they almost always discover:

  • 20% of clients generate 80%+ of profit
  • Some "big" clients are break-even or negative
  • Small, "easy" clients are often the most profitable per hour
  • Scope creep is destroying margin on specific accounts

Real example: A client thought their largest customer was their best. Analysis showed that client was actually #7 in profitability—they'd negotiated deep discounts and demanded constant extra work. The #1 most profitable client was less than half the revenue.

What to Do With This Information

  • Profitable clients: Protect them. Ask for referrals. Do more of what they need.
  • Break-even clients: Raise prices, reduce scope, or improve efficiency.
  • Unprofitable clients: Fix it, fire them, or accept it as a strategic loss leader.

Bottom Line

  • Measure profit by client, not just revenue
  • Include ALL hours, not just billed hours
  • Your biggest client might be your worst
  • This analysis often reveals where to focus (and what to stop)
