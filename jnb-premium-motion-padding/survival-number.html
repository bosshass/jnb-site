The Survival Number

What your business needs monthly just to stay alive — before profit, before growth.

Floor Rate Isn't Enough

In Module 1, you learned your floor rate — what you need to charge per billable hour just to cover the cost of that person. But that doesn't tell you if the business survives.

The survival number answers a different question: How much cash does the business need to bring in every month just to keep the lights on?

What Goes Into It

Your survival number includes everything that has to get paid regardless of how much work you do:

  • Fixed costs: Rent, insurance, software, equipment leases, utilities
  • Payroll: Everyone's wages + taxes + benefits (including you)
  • Debt service: Loan payments, equipment financing, lines of credit
  • Owner's draw: What you need to live — mortgage, car payment, groceries
  • Taxes: Quarterly estimates, sales tax liability

The Calculation

Monthly Survival Number = Fixed Costs + Payroll + Debt Service + Owner's Draw + Tax Reserve

Example

Small service business with 3 employees:

  • Fixed costs: $4,200/mo (rent, insurance, software, utilities)
  • Payroll (3 techs + office): $18,500/mo
  • Debt service: $1,200/mo
  • Owner's draw: $6,000/mo
  • Tax reserve (15%): calculated on profit

Monthly survival number: $29,900

That's the minimum that has to hit the bank every single month before anything else matters.

What's Actually Left

Here's the part nobody talks about: after you hit survival, what's left for reinvestment? For emergencies? For growth?

If you're bringing in $35,000/month against a $29,900 survival number, your actual margin is $5,100. One bad month, one lost contract, one big repair — and you're underwater.

Bottom Line

  • Survival number = everything the business needs to stay alive
  • It includes owner's draw — you need to eat too
  • Revenue above survival is your actual margin for growth
  • If you don't know this number, you don't know if you're profitable
